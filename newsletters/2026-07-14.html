🧪 GPT-5.6 Sol out-plans Fable 5 on three systems
Across three fresh backend system-design tasks, GPT-5.6 Sol averaged 9.10 to Fable 5's 8.04 on our planning rubric. Last month Fable 5 beat GPT-5.5 9.1 to 8.3 on a single feature-flag service. Sol is the first flagship we've tested since, and it closed the gap.
The interesting difference is behavior, not raw quality. After writing each plan, Sol launched sub-agents to audit its own work and kept revising based on what they found, taking 3.4x longer and costing about 13% more than Fable. Fable produced leaner, more buildable plans in roughly 15 minutes each. Both are available in Kilo today, so the tradeoff is yours to make.
Why planning matters: once a plan exists, frontier models implement it almost interchangeably. Planning is still where they actually differ.

🔐 We took the keys out of the box
Cloud Agents no longer carry your real GitHub or Kilo tokens into the sandbox. Your real credentials stay in our control plane. The sandbox gets a short-lived, sandbox-specific capability token instead, encrypted, scoped to a single container, and useless anywhere else.
Every outbound request goes through three checks before it leaves the network. We decrypt the token to confirm we issued it, confirm it's going to a destination we allow (GitHub tokens only reach GitHub, Kilo tokens only reach Kilo), and confirm it's coming from the sandbox we issued it to. If all three pass, we swap the capability token for the real one for that single request and forward it. Real tokens never sit in the sandbox's environment, never get written to disk, and never get logged anywhere the agent could read.
GitLab support is coming next, built on the same architecture. This is live now for every Cloud Agent session.

📊 Fable 5 before and after the ban: what the data shows
When Fable 5 came back on July 1, we re-ran the same 445-session KiloBench suite we ran on launch day. The headline intervention rate barely moved — 88 sessions on June 9, 98 on July 4. The interesting number is what intervention looks like now.
Of the 98 flagged sessions in the July run, only 17 saw a refusal. The other 81 silently fell back to Opus 4.8. Measured at the step level, 861 of the run's 5,408 steps were served by Opus — roughly one in six. In 53 sessions, every single step routed to Opus, including sessions that ran 31, 27, and 25 steps. You ask for Fable, the session runs to completion, and Fable never touches it.
Independent benchmark numbers published for "Fable 5" this month may partially measure Opus 4.8. That's why some third-party evals showed dramatic drops that say more about the classifier than about Fable's reasoning. If a task category reliably triggers fallback, the right move is to pin it to Opus directly and skip the uncertainty.
