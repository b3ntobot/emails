🆕 New: Kilo for JetBrains

Kilo is now available as a native plugin for IntelliJ, PyCharm, and other JetBrains IDEs, built in Kotlin/Swing specifically for the JetBrains platform rather than shipped as a webview wrapper or VS Code port. The first release includes chat, slash commands, file mentions, model and provider configuration, MCP server support, and attachments, with some specialized VS Code features still being ported.

🔍 You can finally see what Auto is doing with your bill
Auto routing just got a transparency upgrade. Pick Auto Frontier or Auto Efficient and every turn of your session now shows which model actually handled the request, right inside the task. You can already see model usage on the usage dashboard — this puts the same info next to the work itself, so it's obvious which kinds of requests need a frontier model and which don't.
Under the hood, KiloBench got a fresh batch of runs. More models, more recent data, and routing decisions backed by benchmark numbers that are days old, not months old. Combined, that means Auto Efficient keeps delivering on its 71% of published frontier completion at 72% lower cost claim, and you can actually audit which model did what.

🐉 Tencent's Hy3 is now generally available
Hy3 Preview shook up our workflows during the trial — the GA release is here, and it's free for a limited time across every Kilo surface. Tencent scaled up the RL training and tightened the post-training data, and the result lands hard on real coding work. In blind tests with expert workflows, Hy3 consistently outperformed similar-sized open models and went toe-to-toe with open-source flagships at 2–5x the parameter count.
The headline numbers: hallucination rate dropped from 12.5% to 5.4%, tool-call success and error recovery got a real upgrade, and multi-turn intent tracking holds up over long refactoring sessions. It works in VS Code, the CLI, and Kilo Cloud agents, with high limits thanks to compute from Novita while the free period runs.

💼 AI is creating jobs. The compute bill is the real story.
The data doesn't match the doom headlines. Ramp and Revelio Labs linked spending data to workforce records for 21,559 U.S. firms and found the heaviest AI adopters grew headcount 10% over the two years after adoption, with entry-level hiring up 12%. Box's separate survey of 1,640 companies landed on the same pattern: 58% expect headcount to rise, climbing to 79% among the most AI-mature firms, and only 9% say agents are primarily eliminating roles.
The tension nobody's pricing in: the five companies funding most of the compute have seen combined free cash flow fall nearly 90% in 18 months. Frontier labs are heading toward some of the largest IPOs in history, and their valuations assume token consumption keeps climbing. That pressure trickles down to your coding bill. The takeaway for engineering teams: plan for the bill, not the press release.

🎤 What 23 developer events in 6 months actually taught us
The Kilo DevRel team spent the first half of 2026 in the rooms where developers actually are. Hackathons, enterprise summits, open source conferences, and a few happy hours in NYC — across Spain, London, Miami, Las Vegas, and back. In total, 3,000+ conversations with developers, and the takeaways are sharper than the conference circuit usually produces.
The single most jarring thing was the gap between rooms. At power-user events, fifteen-year-olds are running $30k/month businesses on top of agentic tooling and people have automated their entire workday down to a 10-minute check-in. A week later, at a corporate conference, experienced developers are still figuring out how to get good results out of ChatGPT. The adoption gap is wider than anything the team has seen before, and that's both a problem and an opening.
